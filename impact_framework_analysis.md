## The Impact Measurement Framework

### 4 Impact Measurement Pillars

These are "the foundation of our (new) data architecture" - the categories that help measure impact:

1. **Influence**
   - Metrics showing YWCA's unique position as local + national presence
   - Uplifting voices of beneficiaries
   - Pushing for policies that improve lives

2. **Research**
   - Groundbreaking research and insights
   - Changing how society thinks about equity

3. **Local Relevance**
   - Meeting communities where they are
   - Giving LAs support, guidance, flexibility
   - Making difference on the ground
   - Responding to community needs in real time

4. **Collective Disruption**
   - Bringing together best insights from local entities at national level
   - Offering differentiated point of view on gender and racial equity

**Platform Implication:** The platform needs to tag/categorize data by these pillars to enable analysis.

---

### 3 Types of Impact (What They Want to Measure)

#### 1. Societal Impacts (Community/Cultural Level)
Shifts in social norms, behaviors, attitudes, or conditions that benefit community as a whole:

- Entities in local communities demonstrate **shift in communication** about a social problem
- Shift in communication leads to **reduction/elimination of social denial**
- Local government/administrative body **changes or adopts policy** reflecting YWCA goals
- **YWCA positioned as leader** in developing solutions

#### 2. Beneficiary Impacts (Individual Level)
Positive changes for individuals receiving services directly:

- Increased **access or ability to secure basic needs**
- Increased **confidence and/or skills**
- Increased **sense of belonging and/or safety**

#### 3. Institutional Impacts (Systems Level)
Changes in institutions that influence policies and practices:

- Institutions **exhibit more meaningful engagement** on social issues
- Institutions **recognize their role** in perpetrating harm
- Institutions **assume responsibility and take explicit steps** to undo harm

**Platform Implication:** Programs and organizational efforts need to be linked to these impact types for reporting and analysis.

## Violence Types Framework

### Racial Violence
**Definition:** The systematic mistreatment and degradation of specific groups of people, based on race; supported and enforced by society and its institutions.

**Examples of Interventions:**
- Undoing impacts of redlining
- Undoing systemic biases through narrative change
- Combating stereotypes, biases, racial power dynamics
- Training on narrative change
- Training groups targeted by racism to manage and overcome it
- Research on harms of racism
- Advancing environmental justice at intersection of racial justice
- Defending and protecting equal access to voting
- Providing basic needs for a specific racial group
- Voter registration and education

### Gender-Based Violence
**Definition:** The systematic mistreatment and degradation of specific groups of people, based on gender; supported and enforced by society and its institutions.

**Examples of Interventions:**
- Improving maternal health outcomes
- Access to affordable childcare
- Closing the gender pay or wealth gap
- Advocating for reproductive rights and freedoms
- Women's empowerment
- Intimate partner violence protection and prevention
- Advancing environmental justice at intersection of gender-based justice

### Economic Violence
**Definition:** A systemic attitude, pattern of thinking, practice, process, or policy directed by institutions toward people whose social status is devalued because characteristics intrinsic to their identities differ from what is valued by those in power. Often results in socioeconomic damaging of a community's wealth-building capability.

**Examples of Interventions:**
- Guaranteed income programs
- Access to financial institutions and services
- Workforce development and training
- Providing financial literacy/education
- Providing entrepreneurship education and training
- Providing leadership programming
- Securing access to housing
- Enhancing ability to build assets
- Providing basic needs for all (not just a specific group)
- Undoing how environmental injustices disproportionately affect lower socioeconomic strata

**Platform Implication:** Programs need structured tagging by violence type, and the platform should suggest/validate intervention types based on violence type selected.

## Key Definitions (Data Dictionary for Platform)

### Organizational Terms

**Full-time worker**: Someone who works at least 30 hours per week and will receive a W-2 or 1099. May include 1099 contractors.

**Part-time worker**: Someone who works less than 20 hours per week and will receive a W-2 or 1099.

**Volunteer**: Someone who serves the organization without compensation.

**Annual revenue**: IRS "gross receipts" on 990 - total amounts received from all sources during tax year, without subtracting costs/expenses.

**Social problem**: A social condition or set of conditions that is troublesome, prevalent, can be changed, and should be changed.

**Strategic plan**: Document outlining organization's goals, strategies, efforts, activities, and actions to achieve those goals.

### Program-Related Terms

**Organizational effort**: Initiatives or activities organization undertakes (e.g., advocacy work leading to policy change) NOT associated with a program.

**Program**: A set of planned activities or services that the organization provides to achieve its intended impact and advance its mission.

**Recent and/or active program**: Programs implemented from 2022 to present.

**Stated objective or purpose**: The specific goal or intended outcome that a program aims to achieve through stated activities.

**Program activity**: Specific actions, tasks, and/or steps taken to implement program's objectives, directly contributing to desired outcome (e.g., trainings, various forms of assistance).

### Impact-Related Terms

**Intended impact**: The tangible effect we hope to have or declare we will have on our beneficiaries, communities, and broader society as a result of our organizational efforts, programs, and core values.

**Societal impact**: Positive changes or outcomes at community/cultural level, involving shifts in social norms, behaviors, attitudes, or conditions that benefit community as whole, not just individual beneficiaries.

**Beneficiary impact**: Positive changes or outcomes experienced by individuals or groups who receive a program's services directly.

**Institutional impact**: Positive changes or outcomes in local institutions that influence their policies and practices.

**Evidence**: Information, data, or documentation that demonstrates the program has achieved intended outcomes or made significant progress toward goals.

### Support Infrastructure Terms

**Tools**: Something that enables you to effectively measure, manage, and execute programs and activities (e.g., research software, collaboration platforms, data analysis, volunteer management systems).

**Supports**: Assistance and resources that help enhance organization's capacity and effectiveness (e.g., legal and compliance support, changed or adopted policy).

**Systems**: An organized set of procedures and processes that help make decisions to achieve intended impact (e.g., pre/post-surveys, focus groups, community partnerships).

## What This Means for Platform Design

### 1. Structured Taxonomies
The platform needs controlled vocabularies/taxonomies for:
- Violence types (racial, gender-based, economic)
- Impact types (societal, beneficiary, institutional)
- Impact measurement pillars (influence, research, local relevance, collective disruption)
- Evidence types (policy adoption, pre/post surveys, media, funding, etc.)
- Program activity types (varies by violence type)

### 2. Research Capabilities
This isn't just data storage - it's research infrastructure:
- Statistical analysis (correlations, regressions)
- Cross-tabulations
- Filtering by multiple variables
- Export to research tools
- Possibly integration with statistical software (R, SPSS, Stata)

### 3. Data Validation
Inputs should be validated against the framework:
- If program addresses "racial violence," suggest appropriate intervention types
- If program claims "institutional impact," require appropriate evidence types
- Ensure coherence between violence type, activities, and impacts

### 4. Built-in Definitions
Every field should have tooltip/help text with official definitions from glossary

### 5. Flexible Framework
Note says impact areas "may need more specificity as we learn more" - the system should allow:
- Adding new impact areas
- Adding new violence types
- Adding new intervention types
- Without requiring code changes (configuration-driven)

## Platform Design Challenge

**The core tension:**
- Make it simple enough for a 2-person volunteer chapter to use
- Make it sophisticated enough to support academic research
- Make it operational enough for grant writing
- Make it analytical enough for correlational studies

**The solution:**
- **UI Layer:** Simple, intuitive, "data intranet" feel
- **Data Layer:** Structured, validated, research-quality
- **Reporting Layer:** Multiple outputs (grant reports for chapters, research exports for 10Seven, policy briefs for HQ)
- **Analysis Layer:** Statistical tools available but not required
